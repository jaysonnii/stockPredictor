How to tell if a stock is overvalued or undervalued

The most common beginner method is the Benjamin Graham formula — a simple way to estimate what a stock is actually worth based on its earnings, then compare that to the current price.

Intrinsic Value = EPS × (8.5 + 2 × growth rate)

EPS = Earnings Per Share (how much profit per share)
8.5 = Graham's base P/E for a no-growth company
growth rate = expected annual earnings growth (%)

If the intrinsic value is higher than the current price → the stock may be undervalued (potentially a good buy).

If the intrinsic value is lower than the current price → the stock may be overvalued (you're paying a premium).

Your app calculates this automatically on the Overview tab using the stock's current EPS and a 10% assumed growth rate.

How to read a price chart

A price chart shows you how a stock's value has moved over time. Here's what to look for:

  • Trend direction — is the line going up (bullish), down (bearish), or sideways (consolidating)? Buying into an uptrend is generally safer.
  • Support level — a price the stock keeps bouncing off from below. If it holds, that's a potential buying zone.
  • Resistance level — a price the stock keeps failing to break above. If it breaks through, that's a bullish signal.
  • Sharp drops — sudden dips can be buying opportunities OR warning signs. Check the news to find out why.
  • 52-week range — if the stock is near its 52W low, it's pulled back a lot. Near its 52W high means it's been on a run.
  • Zoom out — always look at the 1Y and 5Y charts, not just 1M. Short-term noise can look scary but be meaningless long-term.

Key ratios explained

P/E Ratio
Price-to-Earnings. How much you're paying for every $1 of the company's profit.
S&P 500 avg ~25. Under 15 = cheap. Over 40 = expensive or high-growth expected.
EPS
Earnings Per Share. The company's profit divided by number of shares. Higher = more profitable.
Rising EPS over time is a good sign. Negative EPS means the company is losing money.
Market Cap
Total value of all shares combined. Tells you the size of the company.
Large cap >$10B = stable. Mid cap $2–10B = growth potential. Small cap <$2B = higher risk.
52W High / Low
The highest and lowest price the stock hit in the past year.
Near the low can mean buying opportunity. Near the high means momentum — but could reverse.
Volume
How many shares were traded that day. High volume = strong conviction behind a move.
A price rise on high volume is more meaningful than on low volume.
Dividend Yield
Annual dividend payment as a % of the stock price. Passive income from holding shares.
2–4% is typical. Very high yields (>7%) can signal a struggling company.

Beginner stock buying checklist

Before buying any stock, ask yourself these questions:

  • Do I understand what this company actually does and how it makes money?
  • Is the P/E ratio reasonable compared to the industry average?
  • Has revenue and EPS been growing over the past 3–5 years?
  • Is the stock near a 52-week low (potential value) or near all-time highs (paying a premium)?
  • What is the overall market trend? Buying in a bear market is riskier.
  • Does the company have significant debt? (Debt-to-equity ratio)
  • Would I be comfortable holding this for 3–5 years if it dropped 30%?
  • Am I investing money I don't need in the next 1–2 years?
  • Have I read recent news or earnings reports about this company?
  • Is this a single stock bet or part of a diversified portfolio?

If you can't answer most of these confidently, consider an ETF like SPY or QQQ first to build exposure while you learn.
